The Story of Unintended Consequences

How a fight against infant mortality lead to the arsenic poisoning of millions

Flickr

The West Bengal Background

Through the 1970's an undeveloped water infrastructure (purification and sewage systems), plus the regular monsoon flood, led to bacterial contamination of water supplies. The extremely poor water quality contributed to one of the highest infant mortality rates in the world. Google

The Original "Solution"

Organizations like UNICEF and the World Bank advocated the use of tube wells, which now average about 100ft in depth, to avoid contaminated surface water. The New York Times Indian Academy of Sciences

The Outcome

While a 50% reduction in infant mortality and diarrheal illness occurred over the past few decades, mass poisoning of the populace by arsenic, which is naturally occurring in the underground sediment of the Ganges Delta, also followed. University of Michigan

A New Approach

In order to reduce the harm from current water sources, collecting and disseminating information is the first step. Lumin

This low cost, convenient test to assess the amount of arsenic in a water sample will facilitate broad and often testing.
